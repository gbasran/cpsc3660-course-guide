PAGE 12; EXAM PREP

The Professor's Signature

A forensic reading of four assignments and one midterm; what keeps coming back, what is coming next, and how the final is almost certainly going to be built.

Physical analogy; a tennis coach watching the rival's serve.

Before any match, a good coach does not just drill; he pulls up three years of footage of the opponent. He watches the toss, the stance, the placement of every serve, and he notices that this opponent hits the T-slot 78% of the time on deuce-side second serves under pressure. That knowledge changes practice the next day; the player drills returning that exact shot. The final exam works the same way. The prof is not random; he has a toolbox of four or five question archetypes that he reaches for every single time. The assignments and midterm are three years of game footage. This page reads them back to you so that when you sit down at 9 AM on exam day, you already know where the ball is going.

Part A: The Opening 5-6 Concept Questions

Every assessment opens with a short block of conceptual definitions; each worth exactly 2 marks, expected to fit on 2 lines. The prof uses these as warm-ups and as grade buoys (everyone who studied can earn 10-12 marks before breaking a sweat). On the final, expect this block to expand to 6-8 questions (12-16 marks) because it must now include post-midterm territory.

The five recurring themes.
Theme 1
DML vs DDL. "Define DML and DDL and give one example of each." Appears in A1 Q1 and on the midterm.
Theme 2
Key type distinction. Define super key, candidate key, primary key; give example. A1 Q3, Midterm Q2.
Theme 3
DBMS components. Name the three subsystems and what each does (storage manager, query processor, data dictionary). Recurring.
Theme 4
RA set-op transformations. Show that $r \cap s = r - (r - s)$. A1 Q4, Midterm Q6.
Theme 5
Integrity / cascading. "What happens if we delete E01 with ON DELETE CASCADE?" Midterm Q7.

Final-Exam Expansion

The midterm had zero marks on ER, FDs, or normalization. The final cannot ignore half the syllabus, so this opening block will grow to include:

  • BCNF definition (2 marks) paired with "when is a decomposition in BCNF?"; mirrors A4 Q2.
  • FD definition (2 marks); "define a functional dependency"; mirrors A4 Q1.
  • Strong vs weak entity (2 marks) plus one example. Mirrors A3 Q5.

Budget: 18 minutes total on this block. These are gift marks; do not overthink them.

Part B: SQL Graduation Pattern

Every SQL question the prof writes has the same staircase. Part (a) is a gift, part (d) is a discriminator, and parts (b) and (c) are the GROUP BY exercises that separate B-students from A-students.

The graduation, with example difficulty.
(a) Basic
Simple SELECT + WHERE; 2 marks; "find employees with age > 30". Gift tier; never missed by a prepared student.
(b) Medium
GROUP BY with an aggregate; 2-3 marks; "count books per publisher". Requires group by publisher and count(*).
(c) Medium
GROUP BY + HAVING + threshold; 3 marks; "branches where avg balance > 10000". Requires HAVING not WHERE.
(d) Hard
Correlated subquery or NOT EXISTS; 3-4 marks; "employees earning more than avg in their own company"; or "employees who borrowed ALL books by Springer".

Why (c) is guaranteed to appear

GROUP BY + HAVING with an average threshold has been on every SQL-based assessment:

A1

Q5: "branches with average account balance above 10000" (Bank schema).

A2

Q4: "publishers whose books have average borrow count over 5" (Library schema, group-having variant).

A3

Q1(c): "for every company, find cities where avg salary exceeds a threshold" (Employee-Works schema).

Midterm

Q5(b): "publishers with average borrows > 3" (Library).

Probability on the final: 95%. Practice the template until it is muscle memory.

The GROUP BY + HAVING template
select <group-attr>, avg(<num-attr>)
from <table>
group by <group-attr>
having avg(<num-attr>) > <threshold>;

Part C: RA Multi-Part Question Anatomy

Every assessment has one dedicated relational-algebra question, always 9-12 marks, always split into 3-4 parts. The structure is extraordinarily stable.

RA question skeleton.
Part (a)
Selection + projection + natural join; 2 marks medium. "Find names of employees who borrowed a book." $\pi_{name}(employee \bowtie borrow)$.
Part (b)
Aggregation in RA; 3 marks hard. Uses $\gamma$ (or the $\mathcal{G}$ calligraphic form in the slides). "For each publisher, count the number of books."
Part (c)
Division / universal quantification; 3 marks hard. The classic "borrowed ALL books from publisher X" phrasing. Uses $\div$.
Part (d)
CREATE TABLE DDL; 2 marks medium. Often paired with the same schema, asking you to write the CREATE with keys, FKs, and constraints.

Division is the discriminator

On every RA question the prof writes, there is one "for all" or "every" clue word that tells you division is in play. "Employees who borrowed every book by Springer." "Customers who have an account at all branches in Brooklyn." If you see those words and you do not reach for $\div$, you are guessing. See Page 15 for worked division problems.

Part E: Post-Midterm Territory

The midterm had zero marks on ER diagrams, functional dependencies, or normalization. That means the final carries the full weight of those topics: expect 40-45% of the paper to cover them.

Predicted post-midterm question set (total ~48-52 marks).
ER construction
12-15 marks. Full ER design from a natural-language prompt; include entities, relationships, cardinalities, multi-valued attributes, and at least one ISA/generalization. Mirrors A3 Q4 (car insurance) and A3 Q8 (online bookstore with ISA).
Attribute closure
3-4 marks. Given a relation $R$ and a set of FDs $F$, compute $A^+$ under $F$. Also "enumerate all candidate keys of $R$". Mirrors A4 Q3 and A4 Q6(a-b).
Decomposition properties
5-8 marks. Given a proposed decomposition, check lossless-join and dependency preservation. Mirrors A4 Q5.
BCNF decomposition
5-7 marks. Given $R$ and $F$, find the BCNF decomposition using the violation algorithm. Mirrors A4 Q6(c); the "showpiece" question.

The showpiece; A4 Q6 almost certainly returns

A4 Q6 is the only place the prof walked through the full pipeline: candidate keys → BCNF violation → decomposition → lossless check → dependency-preservation check. This entire pipeline is worth 15+ marks when bundled. The final almost certainly features a near-verbatim version; study Page 17 until the algorithm is automatic.

Part G: The Prof's Freebies

Every exam has a predictable set of questions that are free if you did the assignments. Make sure you bank every single one.

Guaranteed easy marks.
Concept definitions
10-16 marks (5-8 Qs at 2m each). Two-line answers. See Page 13 for all 15 phrased and answered.
Simple SELECT + WHERE
2-3 marks. "Find names of employees with age > 30." Three-line query; always the first sub-part of the SQL question.
Simple CREATE TABLE with FK
2-3 marks. Write the CREATE TABLE for one of the recycled schemas. PK, NOT NULL, FK with ON DELETE CASCADE.
Simple GROUP BY
2-3 marks. "Count books per publisher." One group by, one aggregate, no HAVING.

Freebie discipline

These 4 categories together are ~18-25 marks of easy points. If you walk out having lost any of them, you left letter grades on the table. Do every concept question first; the brain is freshest, and you have 12-16 marks in 15 minutes.

Part H: The Prof's Differentiators

These are the questions that sort B's from A's. They are always present, always worth 3-7 marks each, and always the place where prepared students separate themselves.

Hard question archetypes.
Division in RA
3-4 marks. "Employees who borrowed ALL books by Springer." Express as $\pi_{name,isbn}(\sigma \ldots) \div \pi_{isbn}(\ldots)$. See Page 15 Problem 3.
NOT EXISTS for universal quantification
3-4 marks. SQL equivalent of division; "there does not exist a Springer book that this employee has not borrowed." See Page 14 Problem 6.
BCNF decomposition (full pipeline)
5-7 marks. Find candidate keys, identify violation, decompose, check lossless, check dependency preservation. See Page 17 Walkthrough 1.
Armstrong's inference (proof)
3 marks. "Prove pseudo-transitivity from reflexivity, augmentation, transitivity." Line-by-line numbered proof with rule justifications.

The division-trap

Students who confuse division with "set difference" lose full marks on RA Q(c). The tell: if the question mentions "every" or "all" across two sets, use $\div$; if it mentions "except" or "not in," use $-$. The prof has never once tested "except" on a final; he always tests "all".

Glossary

Graduation pattern
The prof's habit of asking (a) basic, (b)-(c) medium, (d) hard sub-parts within a single SQL or RA question.
Discriminator
A hard question designed to separate top grades from average grades. Worth 3-7 marks.
Freebie
An easy question that every prepared student should bank (concept defs, simple SELECT, simple CREATE TABLE).
Recycled schema
A schema (Library, Bank, Employee-Works-Company, University, Online Bookstore) that the prof uses across multiple assessments.
Post-midterm territory
ER model, functional dependencies, normalization. Absent from the midterm; expected ~40% of the final.
Showpiece
The BCNF decomposition + lossless + dependency-preservation question; the biggest mark cluster on the final.

3 Full ER Design Walkthroughs

Car insurance, online bookstore with ISA, and a novel hospital scenario. Each walked from natural-language prompt to schema.

Physical analogy; a pathologist reading an X-ray.

A pathologist does not stare at an X-ray and freestyle a diagnosis; she follows a checklist: scan for the big shapes first (heart, lungs, spine), then look for symmetry, then look for specific abnormalities one region at a time. ER design works the same way: scan for the nouns (entities), then the verbs (relationships), then the cardinalities, then the ISA patterns, then the weak entities. The discipline is the checklist; the creativity is filling it in. These three exercises walk through the checklist on real problems.
The ER checklist.
  1. Identify entities (nouns that have attributes of their own).
  2. Identify relationships (verbs between entities).
  3. Assign cardinalities (1:1, 1:N, M:N).
  4. Identify multi-valued, composite, and derived attributes.
  5. Identify ISA / generalization (shared attribute subsets).
  6. Identify weak entities (no natural standalone key).
  7. Draw the diagram.
  8. Map to relations (one table per entity, one per M:N relationship, flatten 1:N into the "many" side).

Exercise 1: Car Insurance (A3 Q4 verbatim)

The domain. An insurance company tracks customers, cars, and insurance policies. A customer owns one or more cars; each car has exactly one owner (at a time). Each car has one or more insurance types attached to it. Start from the requirements and walk through the whole pipeline.
Requirements (verbatim from A3 Q4).
  • Customer: CID (unique), name, phone, email.
  • Car: VID (unique), brand, year, price.
  • Insurance: IID (unique), type, price, start_date, end_date.
  • Owns: each customer owns 1+ cars; each car has exactly 1 owner.
  • Insured_by: each car has 1+ insurance instances; one insurance instance may apply to many cars.
Step 1 Underline nouns in the prompt: Customer, Car, Insurance. Each has its own attributes → these are entity sets.
Step 2 Verbs describe relationships: owns (Customer–Car), insured_by (Car–Insurance). Each becomes a diamond.
Step 3 Ask the cardinality question from each side. "How many cars can one customer own?" → many. "How many owners can one car have?" → one. So Customer 1 — N Car.
Step 4 Same for insurance: "Can one car have many policies?" yes. "Can one policy cover many cars?" yes. So Car M — N Insurance.
Step 5 Identify PKs (CID, VID, IID); underline each on paper. No weak entities here (every noun has its own key). No ISA.
Step 6 Map to relations: 1:N collapses into the many side; M:N gets its own junction table.
Entities: Customer, Car, Insurance
Rels: owns (Cust-Car), insured_by (Car-Ins)
Cards: Cust 1--N Car ; Car M--N Ins
Keys: CID, VID, IID (all underlined)
Attrs: Cust{name, phone, email} ; Car{brand, year, price} ; Ins{type, price, start, end}
→ tables: customer, car(+owner_CID fk), insurance, car_insurance(junction)

Schema mapping:

customer(CID, name, phone, email)
car(VID, brand, year, price, owner_CID)   // 1:N merged in
   fk owner_CID → customer(CID)
insurance(IID, type, price, start_date, end_date)
car_insurance(VID, IID)                 // M:N junction
   fk VID → car(VID)
   fk IID → insurance(IID)
Verify: every car has an owner (owner_CID NOT NULL); one cust → many cars; one car → many policies via junction.

Step-by-step design

Step 1: Identify entities

Three nouns with their own attributes: Customer, Car, Insurance.

Step 2: Identify relationships and cardinalities

Owns: Customer 1 — N Car; total participation on Car (every car has exactly one owner).
Insured_by: Car M — N Insurance (many-to-many); total participation on Car (every car is insured by something).

Step 3: Draw the ER diagram
  +------------+       1    owns    N     +--------+
  |  Customer  |<---------- <> ----------->|  Car   |
  +------------+                          +--------+
  CID* name phone email                   VID* brand year price
                                                |
                                                | M
                                              <>insured_by
                                                | N
                                                v
                                           +-----------+
                                           | Insurance |
                                           +-----------+
                                           IID* type price start_date end_date

(*) marks the primary key. <> marks a relationship diamond. On paper, draw rectangles for entities, diamonds for relationships, ovals for attributes; underline PK attributes.

Step 4: Map to relations

One table per entity; the 1:N relationship (owns) is merged into the "many" side (Car), which picks up a FK to Customer. The M:N relationship (insured_by) becomes its own junction table.

customer(CID, name, phone, email)
car(VID, brand, year, price, ownerCID)
  foreign key (ownerCID) references customer(CID)
insurance(IID, type, price, start_date, end_date)
car_insurance(VID, IID)
  foreign key (VID) references car(VID)
  foreign key (IID) references insurance(IID)
Step 5: Verify requirements

Every car has an owner (ownerCID NOT NULL). One customer can own many cars (many rows in car with the same ownerCID). One car can have many insurance types (many rows in car_insurance with the same VID). All checks pass.

Mirrors A3 Q4 verbatim; ~12-15 marks on the final

Exercise 2: Online Bookstore with Generalization (A3 Q8 verbatim)

The domain. An online bookstore initially sells only books. Then the business expands to music (CDs and cassettes). Music items ship the same way books do, so they share a lot of structure. ER generalization (ISA) captures this without duplicating attributes.
Requirements (verbatim from A3 Q8).
  • Book: isbn (unique), title, year, price.
  • Customer: email (unique), name, address.
  • Shopping_basket: basketID (unique), owned by one customer.
  • Warehouse: code (unique), location.
  • Author: name, bio.
  • Publisher: name, address.
  • Relationships: written_by (book M — N author), published_by (book N — 1 publisher), contains (basket M — N book), stocks (warehouse M — N book).
  • Extension: music (cassettes, CDs) shipped like books; has its own attributes (e.g. playlist, format) plus shared attributes (isbn-like identifier, title, price, warehouse stocking).
Step 1 List nouns: Book, Customer, Shopping_basket, Warehouse, Author, Publisher, Music. Seven entity candidates.
Step 2 Spot shared attributes between Book and Music: id, title, price. This is the ISA signal; promote shared attrs to a supertype Item.
Step 3 Verbs → relationships: written_by (M:N), published_by (N:1), contains (M:N), stocks (M:N), owns (1:N Customer–Basket).
Step 4 Spot multi-valued: authors → many per book; modelled as the M:N written_by, not as a multi-valued attr.
Step 5 ISA triangle: Item at top; Book and Music inherit id/title/price from Item and add their own (year for Book, playlist/format for Music).
Step 6 Warehouse stocks Item (not Book); one relationship covers both subtypes.
Entities: Item(parent), Book ISA Item, Music ISA Item, Customer, Basket, Warehouse, Author, Publisher
Shared (→ Item): itemID, title, price
Book-only: year
Music-only: playlist, format
Rels: written_by (Book M—N Author) ; published_by (Book N—1 Publisher) ; contains (Basket M—N Item) ; stocks (Warehouse M—N Item) ; owns (Cust 1—N Basket)
→ table per subtype (Option A); Book and Music reuse itemID as PK+FK

Schema mapping (table-per-subtype; Option A):

customer(email, name, address)
item(itemID, title, price)
book(itemID, year)               // PK and fk → item(itemID)
music(itemID, format)             // PK and fk → item(itemID)
music_playlist(itemID, track)     // multi-valued split out
author(name, bio)
publisher(name, address)
basket(basketID, customer_email)   // 1:N owns merged
warehouse(code, location)
written_by(itemID, author_name)
published_by(itemID, publisher_name)  // N:1 merged into item-side
contains(basketID, itemID, quantity)
stocks(code, itemID, quantity)

Step-by-step design

Step 1: Identify entities and classify shared attributes

Book and music share id (isbn-like), title, price. They differ on author/publisher (book) vs playlist/format (music). This is the ISA pattern.

Step 2: Introduce the supertype Item

Promote the shared attributes into a parent entity Item(itemID, title, price). Let Book and Music ISA Item; each subtype inherits Item's attributes and adds its own.

Step 3: Draw the ER fragment around ISA
                    +--------+
                    |  Item  |   itemID* title price
                    +--------+
                        ^
                       / \
                      / \ \          <-- ISA triangle
                     /   \ \
                    /-----\\
                   /       \\
             +--------+  +---------+
             |  Book  |  |  Music  |
             +--------+  +---------+
             year            playlist format

  written_by, published_by        (stocks, contains relations apply to Item)
Step 4: Work out cardinalities

written_by: Book M — N Author (each book may have multiple authors; each author may write many books).
published_by: Book N — 1 Publisher (each book has exactly one publisher).
contains: Shopping_basket M — N Item (a basket has many items, an item appears in many baskets).
stocks: Warehouse M — N Item (with quantity attribute on the relationship).
owns: Customer 1 — N Shopping_basket.

Step 5: Map to relations
customer(email, name, address)
item(itemID, title, price)
book(itemID, year) -- PK and FK into item
music(itemID, playlist, format)
author(name, bio)
publisher(name, address)
shopping_basket(basketID, customer_email)
warehouse(code, location)
written_by(itemID, author_name)
published_by(itemID, publisher_name) -- 1-side merged
contains(basketID, itemID, quantity)
stocks(code, itemID, quantity)

Book and music tables share the itemID PK which is also a FK to item; this is the "table per subtype" mapping of ISA.

Step 6: Verify requirements

Shared attributes (title, price) live once in item; subtype-specific attributes (year for book, playlist for music) live in their subtype table. Warehouse stocks item (not book specifically); one relationship covers both books and music. All checks pass.

Mirrors A3 Q8 verbatim; ISA is post-midterm territory; ~12-15 marks on the final

Exercise 3: Hospital (novel scenario; stretch practice)

The domain. A hospital tracks patients, doctors, appointments, and wards. The prof likes to test novel scenarios with two twists: a reified relationship (appointment) that is rich enough to be an entity, and an optional relationship (patients may or may not be admitted to a ward).
Requirements.
  • Patient: PID (unique), name, dob.
  • Doctor: DID (unique), specialty.
  • Appointment: links patient to doctor; has date and time attributes.
  • Ward: WID (unique), name, capacity.
  • A patient may be admitted to a ward (optional).
  • A doctor may supervise a ward (optional; at most one supervisor per ward).
Step 1 Nouns: Patient, Doctor, Appointment, Ward. Ask: does Appointment stand on its own? It has its own attributes (date, time) → promote to a weak entity (reified relationship).
Step 2 Appointment's key: no unique standalone id → composite (PID, DID, date, time). Double-border the rectangle; dashed-underline the discriminator.
Step 3 Cardinality probe: "Every patient must be admitted?" no → 0..1 on patient side of admitted_to. "Every ward has a supervisor?" no → 0..1 on doctor side of supervises.
Step 4 Identifying relationships have double diamonds. Admitted_to and supervises are plain (not identifying).
Step 5 Map: nullable FKs for the optional 1:N rels; composite-key table for appointment.
Entities: Patient, Doctor, Ward (strong); Appointment (weak, owned by Patient+Doctor)
Rels:
  - Patient —has— Appointment —has— Doctor (identifying)
  - Patient 0..1 —admitted_to— N Ward
  - Doctor 0..1 —supervises— 1 Ward
Keys: PID, DID, WID; appointment = (PID, DID, date, time)
→ tables: patient (+wardWID nullable), doctor, ward (+supervisorDID nullable), appointment (composite PK)

Schema mapping:

patient(PID, name, dob, wardWID)        // wardWID nullable (0..1)
   fk wardWID → ward(WID) on delete set null
doctor(DID, specialty)
ward(WID, name, capacity, supervisorDID) // nullable
   fk supervisorDID → doctor(DID) on delete set null
appointment(PID, DID, date, time)   // weak; composite PK
   fk PID → patient(PID) on delete cascade
   fk DID → doctor(DID) on delete cascade
Partial participation on admitted_to → nullable FK. Weak entity → CASCADE on its identifying owners.

Step-by-step design

Step 1: Identify the tricky entity; appointment

Appointment has its own attributes (date, time) and appears in its own right. It is a reified relationship; treat it as a weak entity owned by (patient, doctor). Its discriminator is (date, time); the full key is (PID, DID, date, time).

Step 2: Cardinalities

Admitted_to: Patient 0..1 — N Ward (optional on patient side).
Supervises: Doctor 0..1 — 1 Ward (each ward has at most one supervising doctor, possibly none; doctors may or may not supervise).
Appointment: weak entity with total participation on both Patient and Doctor (an appointment must involve both).

Step 3: Draw the ER diagram (sketch)
  +---------+      has      +-------------+      has     +---------+
  | Patient |-----<>-------| Appointment |-----<>-------| Doctor  |
  +---------+  (weak ent, double-border)                +---------+
  PID* name dob             date+ time+                 DID* specialty
       |                                                    |
       | 0..1 admitted_to N                                 | 0..1 supervises 1
       v                                                    v
  +--------+                                           +--------+
  | Ward   |                                           | Ward   |
  +--------+                                           +--------+
  WID* name capacity

(+) marks the weak-entity discriminator (dashed underline on paper). Double borders mark the weak entity and its identifying relationship.

Step 4: Map to relations
patient(PID, name, dob, wardWID) -- wardWID nullable
  foreign key (wardWID) references ward(WID) on delete set null
doctor(DID, specialty)
ward(WID, name, capacity, supervisorDID) -- supervisorDID nullable
  foreign key (supervisorDID) references doctor(DID) on delete set null
appointment(PID, DID, date, time)
  foreign key (PID) references patient(PID)
  foreign key (DID) references doctor(DID)
Step 5: Verify

Patient can be admitted to a ward (wardWID set) or not (NULL). Doctor can supervise a ward (supervisorDID set on the ward row) or not. Appointment is a composite-key entity; its tuple is identified by both the patient and doctor together with the date/time. All checks pass.

Alternative modeling choice

Appointment could be a pure ternary relationship (patient, doctor, time) without reification, if date/time were the only attributes. Because it has attributes and can have constraints of its own (e.g. status, duration), most designers promote it to an entity. Either is defensible on the exam if you justify the choice.

Stretch practice; the prof has not used this domain but tests this pattern (reified relationship + optional FK)

Mapping rules cheat-sheet

PatternMapping Rule
Strong entity EOne table E; PK is E's key attribute.
Weak entity W owned by EOne table W; PK is (E.PK, W.discriminator); FK on E.PK.
1:1 relationship E1 — E2Add one FK to either side; both optional.
1:N relationship E1 — N E2Add FK on E1.PK to the "many" side (E2).
M:N relationship E1 — M N E2Create a junction table with (E1.PK, E2.PK) as composite PK, plus any relationship attributes.
Multi-valued attributeSeparate table (entityPK, value); PK is the pair.
Composite attributeFlatten: each leaf becomes its own column in the entity's table.
Derived attributeDo not store; compute via a view or query.
ISA (generalization)Option A (table per subtype, shared PK). Option B (single table with discriminator column). Option C (one table per level). The prof prefers Option A.

Why ER is worth ~15 marks of careful work

ER design is the only exam topic where the answer key has judgement calls. The prof looks for: (1) entities chosen correctly, (2) cardinalities marked on every relationship, (3) PK attributes underlined, (4) weak entities with double borders, (5) ISA drawn with a triangle, (6) the mapping to relations matches the diagram. Messy diagrams lose marks even when the logic is right; use pencil first, then ink over the final version.

Glossary

Entity
A noun with attributes of its own; becomes a rectangle in the ER diagram and (usually) a table in the schema.
Relationship
A verb between entities; becomes a diamond; mapped to a table if M:N, merged into an entity if 1:N.
Cardinality
1:1, 1:N, M:N; written on the edges of the relationship diamond.
Multi-valued attribute
Attribute that takes a set of values; gets its own table.
Composite attribute
Attribute with sub-parts; flattened to leaf columns.
Weak entity
Entity with no natural standalone key; identified by its owner plus a discriminator.
ISA / Generalization
Hierarchy: supertype's attributes are inherited by subtypes; subtypes add their own.
Reified relationship
A relationship that has its own attributes and/or needs to be referenced elsewhere; promoted to a weak entity.
Total participation
Every instance of the entity must participate in the relationship (drawn with a double line).
Partial participation
Some instances may not participate; FK column is nullable.

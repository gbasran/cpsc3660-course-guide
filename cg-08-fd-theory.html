Functional Dependencies and Armstrong's Axioms

The rulebook of database design: how attributes imply other attributes, and how to derive every implication from three core axioms.

A functional dependency is a promise the data makes about itself. Saying $\text{SIN} \to \text{Name}$ is saying "tell me the SIN, and I can tell you the name with certainty; no two people with the same SIN will ever have different names." It is a one-way lookup guarantee, like saying "a postal code fixes a province" (it does) but "a province does not fix a postal code" (it doesn't).

Armstrong's axioms are the three rules of inference for these promises. Given a starting set of FDs (the ones you know from the business domain), the axioms tell you every other FD that must logically hold. They are the Euclid of database design: three postulates, an entire geometry of consequences.

Part A: What a Functional Dependency Is

PART A

Picture a phone book. The entry for each phone number lists one name; if you look up 555-1234 twice, you get the same person both times. That is $\text{Phone} \to \text{Name}$. Now flip it: the entry for each name might list several phone numbers (home, cell, work), so $\text{Name} \to \text{Phone}$ does not hold. FDs are not symmetric; direction matters.

Functional dependency. Let $R$ be a relation schema and $\alpha, \beta \subseteq R$. We write $\alpha \to \beta$ to mean: for every pair of tuples $t_1, t_2$ in any legal instance of $R$, if $t_1[\alpha] = t_2[\alpha]$ then $t_1[\beta] = t_2[\beta]$.

$\alpha \to \beta$
"$\alpha$ functionally determines $\beta$," or "$\beta$ is functionally dependent on $\alpha$"
$\alpha$
the determinant (left-hand side, LHS), a set of attributes
$\beta$
the dependent (right-hand side, RHS), a set of attributes
$t_1[\alpha]$
the values of tuple $t_1$ restricted to the attributes in $\alpha$

FDs are constraints, not observations. An FD describes what must hold in every legal instance, not what happens to hold in one sample instance. If the schema allows two people to share a phone number, then $\text{Phone} \to \text{Name}$ is not a valid FD, even if your current data happens to have unique phones.

Trivial FD. $\alpha \to \beta$ is trivial when $\beta \subseteq \alpha$. Always holds, gives no information. Example: $\text{Name, Age} \to \text{Name}$.

Example: reading FDs off a small table

Consider this instance of Course(course_id, title, dept, credits):

course_idtitledeptcredits
CPSC3660Database SystemsCPSC3
CPSC2620Fundamentals of Programming IICPSC3

Candidate FDs consistent with this instance

$\text{course\_id} \to \text{title}$, $\text{course\_id} \to \text{dept}$, $\text{course\_id} \to \text{credits}$. Also $\text{course\_id} \to \text{title, dept, credits}$ by union.

Does $\text{dept} \to \text{credits}$ hold?

In this sample all CPSC courses are 3 credits, but the schema does not enforce that. Real-world CPSC4959 might be 6 credits. So no, not a valid FD, only a coincidence.

An FD must be grounded in the business rule, not the sample data.

Part B: Armstrong's Three Core Axioms

PART B

Armstrong's axioms are like the three basic moves in algebra (add the same thing to both sides, multiply both sides, transitive equality). Every other valid move is a combination of these three. They are sound (every FD you derive is genuinely true) and complete (every true FD can be derived from the axioms plus your given FDs).

Axiom 1 (Reflexivity). If $\beta \subseteq \alpha$, then $\alpha \to \beta$.

"A set determines any of its own subsets." Gives you all trivial FDs for free.

Axiom 2 (Augmentation). If $\alpha \to \beta$, then $\alpha\gamma \to \beta\gamma$ for any $\gamma$.

"You can add the same attributes to both sides." Notation note: $\alpha\gamma$ means $\alpha \cup \gamma$ (set union of attribute sets).

Axiom 3 (Transitivity). If $\alpha \to \beta$ and $\beta \to \gamma$, then $\alpha \to \gamma$.

"Chains of determinations compose." This is the workhorse; most FD proofs use it.

Family-tree analogy. Think of FDs as bloodlines. Reflexivity says you are related to yourself. Augmentation says if A is related to B, then A-with-cousin is related to B-with-cousin. Transitivity says if A is your parent and B is A's parent, B is your grandparent. From three simple blood rules you can derive any genealogical connection in the whole family tree. Same with FDs: from the axioms, any derivable FD is reachable.

Part C: Three Derived Rules (convenience theorems)

PART C

The derived rules are not new axioms; they are shortcuts that package a common sequence of core axiom applications into one step. Using them on an exam is fine as long as you can prove them from the three core axioms if asked.

Union. If $\alpha \to \beta$ and $\alpha \to \gamma$, then $\alpha \to \beta\gamma$.

Decomposition. If $\alpha \to \beta\gamma$, then $\alpha \to \beta$ and $\alpha \to \gamma$.

Pseudo-transitivity. If $\alpha \to \beta$ and $\beta\gamma \to \delta$, then $\alpha\gamma \to \delta$.

Part D: Proof of Pseudo-Transitivity (A4 Q4)

PART D

This proof is worth memorizing verbatim. It is a four-line ladder: start at the bottom, climb rung by rung, end at the top. Every exam that has asked to "derive a non-trivial FD from the axioms" on this course uses augmentation + transitivity, which is exactly this pattern. If you learn the shape, you can adapt it to any variant the professor throws at you.

Claim. If $\alpha \to \beta$ and $\beta\gamma \to \delta$, then $\alpha\gamma \to \delta$.

Proof (four steps)

Step 1

$\alpha \to \beta$ (given)

Step 2

$\alpha\gamma \to \beta\gamma$ (augmentation on step 1, add $\gamma$ to both sides)

Step 3

$\beta\gamma \to \delta$ (given)

Step 4

$\alpha\gamma \to \delta$ (transitivity on steps 2 and 3: $\alpha\gamma \to \beta\gamma \to \delta$) $\blacksquare$

Four lines, two axioms used (augmentation then transitivity). Memorize this shape.

Exam Pattern

A4 Q4 asked for exactly this proof. A very similar variant (prove union or decomposition) is almost certain to appear on the final. Always label each line with the axiom used and the previous line number. The marker cannot give partial credit for a derivation without the justification column.

Part E: Attribute Closure $\alpha^+$

PART E

Attribute closure answers "given that I know the values of $\alpha$, what other attribute values am I guaranteed to know?" Think of $\alpha$ as a starting set of known facts; each FD in $F$ is a rule of the form "if you know the LHS, you also know the RHS." You keep firing rules until no new facts appear. The final set is $\alpha^+$.

It is the "what can I compute?" closure of a starting point, just like $2, +2$ generates all even numbers by closing under repeated addition.

$\alpha^+$ (attribute closure of $\alpha$ under $F$). The largest set of attributes that $\alpha$ functionally determines, given the FD set $F$.

$\alpha$
starting set of attributes
$F$
the given FD set
$\alpha^+$
result: every attribute reachable from $\alpha$ via FDs in $F$
Algorithm: computeClosure($\alpha$, $F$)
result := $\alpha$ repeat   for each FD $\beta \to \gamma$ in $F$:     if $\beta \subseteq$ result then result := result $\cup\, \gamma$ until result does not change return result

Worked closure: $R = (A, B, C, D, E)$, $F = \{A \to BC, CD \to E, B \to D, E \to A\}$

Compute $A^+$.

Initialize

result = $\{A\}$

Pass 1

$A \to BC$: $A$ is in result, so add $B, C$. result = $\{A, B, C\}$.

$CD \to E$: $CD \not\subseteq$ result (no D yet), skip.

$B \to D$: $B$ is in result, add $D$. result = $\{A, B, C, D\}$.

$E \to A$: $E \not\in$ result, skip.

Pass 2

$CD \to E$: $CD \subseteq$ result now, add $E$. result = $\{A, B, C, D, E\}$.

All five attributes present; further passes add nothing.

Terminate

$A^+ = \{A, B, C, D, E\}$

More closures on the same $F$

$B^+$

Start $\{B\}$. $B \to D$ adds $D$. Now $\{B, D\}$. No other LHS is a subset of $\{B, D\}$ (we would need $A$ or $CD$ or $E$). Stop. $B^+ = \{B, D\}$.

$BC^+$

Start $\{B, C\}$. $B \to D$ adds $D$: $\{B, C, D\}$. $CD \to E$ fires: $\{B, C, D, E\}$. $E \to A$ fires: $\{A, B, C, D, E\}$. Stop. $BC^+$ = all.

$E^+$

Start $\{E\}$. $E \to A$ adds $A$: $\{A, E\}$. Then $A \to BC$ adds $B, C$: $\{A, B, C, E\}$. Then $B \to D$ adds $D$: $\{A, B, C, D, E\}$. Stop. $E^+$ = all.

Three superkeys spotted: $\{A\}, \{B, C\}, \{E\}$.

Part F: Uses of Attribute Closure

PART F

Use 1: test if $\alpha$ is a superkey. Compute $\alpha^+$. If $\alpha^+ = R$ (covers every attribute of the schema), then $\alpha$ is a superkey. If additionally no proper subset of $\alpha$ has this property, $\alpha$ is a candidate key.

Use 2: test if $\alpha \to \beta$ is implied by $F$. Compute $\alpha^+$. If $\beta \subseteq \alpha^+$, then yes, $F \models \alpha \to \beta$. Otherwise no.

Use 3: compute $F^+$ (closure of the FD set). For every non-empty $\alpha \subseteq R$, compute $\alpha^+$. Then for every $\beta \subseteq \alpha^+$, output $\alpha \to \beta$. The collected set is $F^+$, every FD implied by $F$.

$F^+$ is huge. For a schema with $n$ attributes, there are $2^n - 1$ possible LHS sets and up to $2^n$ possible RHS sets per LHS. So $F^+$ is typically astronomical, which is why in practice we only compute specific $\alpha^+$ values as needed. The algorithm above is a reference definition, not something you would ever run end-to-end on a real schema.

Closure used to test an implication (A4 Q1 style)

Given $F = \{A \to B, B \to C\}$, does $F \models A \to C$?

Compute $A^+$

Start $\{A\}$. $A \to B$ adds $B$. $B \to C$ adds $C$. Stop. $A^+ = \{A, B, C\}$.

Check containment

Is $\{C\} \subseteq \{A, B, C\}$? Yes. So $F \models A \to C$.

You can also derive it directly by transitivity, but closure gives you a mechanical, always-works procedure.

Part G: Subset and Symmetry Properties (A4 Q1-Q2)

PART G

Assignment 4 started with easy warm-up questions about whether FDs behave like functions or relations on sets. The key intuitions: FDs respect subset relations on the LHS (more information never hurts, only helps) but they are one-directional (not symmetric).

LHS augmentation is free. If $\alpha \to \beta$ and $\alpha \subseteq \alpha'$, then $\alpha' \to \beta$. Proof: augmentation. Intuition: knowing more than you need is still enough to determine $\beta$.

FDs are not symmetric. $\alpha \to \beta$ does not imply $\beta \to \alpha$. Example: $\text{SIN} \to \text{Name}$ holds but $\text{Name} \to \text{SIN}$ fails (two people can share a name).

RHS decomposition is always valid. $\alpha \to \beta_1 \beta_2 \cdots \beta_k$ iff $\alpha \to \beta_i$ for every $i$. Proof: decomposition in one direction, union in the other.

Why This Matters

Everything in normalization hinges on attribute closure. BCNF violation detection is "compute $\alpha^+$ for every LHS and check whether it covers $R$." Candidate-key enumeration is "find every minimal $\alpha$ with $\alpha^+ = R$." Lossless decomposition testing reduces to a closure check. Dependency preservation testing reduces to a closure check. If you walk into the final knowing how to compute $\alpha^+$ cold, Part E-onward of this guide will feel mechanical.

Exam Pattern

Expect three kinds of FD questions on the final: (1) compute a specific attribute closure ("find $BC^+$"), (2) prove a derived rule like pseudo-transitivity or union from the three core axioms, (3) enumerate all candidate keys of a given $(R, F)$. Parts D-F are the machinery for all three.

Glossary

Functional dependency (FD)
$\alpha \to \beta$: in every legal instance, matching $\alpha$-values force matching $\beta$-values.
Trivial FD
$\alpha \to \beta$ with $\beta \subseteq \alpha$. Always holds.
Armstrong's axioms
Reflexivity, Augmentation, Transitivity. Sound and complete for FD inference.
Union rule
Derived: if $\alpha \to \beta$ and $\alpha \to \gamma$, then $\alpha \to \beta\gamma$.
Decomposition rule
Derived: if $\alpha \to \beta\gamma$, then $\alpha \to \beta$ and $\alpha \to \gamma$.
Pseudo-transitivity
Derived: if $\alpha \to \beta$ and $\beta\gamma \to \delta$, then $\alpha\gamma \to \delta$.
Attribute closure $\alpha^+$
Largest set of attributes $\alpha$ functionally determines under $F$.
$F^+$
Closure of the FD set $F$; every FD implied by $F$.
Superkey
Attribute set $\alpha$ with $\alpha^+ = R$.
Candidate key
Minimal superkey: no proper subset is a superkey.
Sound
Every FD derivable from the axioms genuinely holds.
Complete
Every FD that must hold can be derived from the axioms.

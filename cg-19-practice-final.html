Part A: Concept Openers (Q1 to Q6; 12 marks)

Question 1 2 marks

Explain the difference between DML and DDL. Give one example of each.

Reveal Solution
Q1:
DDL = Data Definition Lang → schema. // containers
  ex: create, alter, drop, truncate.
DML = Data Manipulation Lang → data. // contents
  ex: insert, update, delete, select.
Mirrors: Midterm Q1, A1 Q1 (recurring opener). Difficulty: Basic.

Your Answer

DDL (Data Definition Language) modifies the database schema. Examples: CREATE, ALTER, DROP, TRUNCATE.

DML (Data Manipulation Language) modifies the data inside existing tables. Examples: INSERT, UPDATE, DELETE, SELECT.

Understanding

DDL answers "what is the shape of the container?" and DML answers "what is inside the container?". The prof always wants one word on the distinction (schema vs data) plus at least one concrete keyword on each side.

Question 2 2 marks

What is the difference between a superkey, a candidate key, and a primary key? Can a relation have more than one candidate key?

Reveal Solution
Q2:
superkey: any attr-set that uniquely IDs a tuple.
candidate key: a minimal superkey (no proper subset works).
primary key: the CK the designer picks as main ID.
Yes — can have many CKs. // e.g. employee(SIN, emp_id, name): {SIN}, {emp_id} both CKs
Hierarchy: superkey ⊇ candidate key ⊇ primary key.
Mirrors: Midterm Q2, A1 Q3. Difficulty: Basic.

Your Answer

Superkey: any set of attributes whose values uniquely identify each tuple. Candidate key: a minimal superkey (no proper subset is also a superkey). Primary key: the candidate key the designer chooses as the main identifier.

Yes, a relation can have several candidate keys. Example: in employee(SIN, emp_id, name), both {SIN} and {emp_id} are candidate keys; one is chosen as the PK.

Understanding

Hierarchy: superkey $\supseteq$ candidate key $\supseteq$ primary key. Every candidate key is a superkey; not every superkey is a candidate key.

Question 3 2 marks

In an E-R diagram, what distinguishes a strong entity set from a weak entity set? Give one example of each.

Reveal Solution
Q3:
Strong: has its own PK; exists on its own. // single rectangle
  ex: loan(loan_number, amount)
Weak: no standalone PK → ID = owner PK + discriminator. // double rectangle
  ex: payment(payment_no, date, amt) owned by loan
  full key = (loan_number, payment_no)
→ identifying rel = double diamond; weak entity always has total part. (double line).
Mirrors: A3 Q5, A3 Q7 (loan/payment). Difficulty: Basic.

Your Answer

A strong entity has its own primary key and exists independently. Example: loan(loan_number, amount); the PK is loan_number.

A weak entity has no primary key of its own; its identifier is the owner PK plus a discriminator unique within that owner. Example: payment(payment_no, date, amt) depends on loan; (loan_number, payment_no) together identify a payment.

Understanding

Visual: strong = single rectangle, weak = double rectangle; identifying relationship is a double diamond; weak entity has total participation (double line) in that relationship.

Question 4 2 marks

Using only the basic relational algebra operations ($\sigma, \pi, \times, \cup, -$), express the intersection $r \cap s$.

Reveal Solution
Q4: express r ∩ s using only primitives // ∩ is derived

  r ∩ s = r − (r − s)
reasoning: (r−s) = tuples in r not in s. Subtract that from r → exactly what r and s share.
6 primitives: σ, π, ×, ∪, −, ρ.
Mirrors: A1 Q4, Midterm Q6. Difficulty: Basic.

Your Answer

$$r \cap s = r - (r - s).$$

Understanding

$r - s$ is "everything in $r$ but not in $s$". Subtracting that from $r$ leaves exactly the tuples $r$ shares with $s$, which is the intersection. The operator $\cap$ is derived, not primitive. The six primitives are $\sigma, \pi, \times, \cup, -, \rho$.

Question 5 2 marks

Define BCNF. Why do we aim for BCNF in database design?

Reveal Solution
Q5: BCNF definition + motivation

R is in BCNF iff for every non-trivial FD α → β in F+,
  α is a superkey of R.
Why? Any FD whose LHS is not a SK causes RHS to repeat → update/insert/delete anomalies. // BCNF kills them structurally.
(trivial = β ⊆ α → exempt)
Mirrors: A4 Q2, expanded for final scope. Difficulty: Basic.

Your Answer

A relation $R$ is in Boyce-Codd Normal Form iff for every non-trivial FD $\alpha \to \beta$ in $F^+$, $\alpha$ is a superkey of $R$.

We aim for BCNF because any FD whose LHS is not a superkey causes redundancy (the RHS repeats), which creates update, insert, and delete anomalies. BCNF eliminates those anomalies structurally.

Understanding

One-liner the prof wants: "For every non-trivial FD, the LHS must be a superkey." Trivial means $\beta \subseteq \alpha$; those are exempt.

Question 6 2 marks

What does the functional dependency $\alpha \to \beta$ mean? Give a counterexample showing that $\alpha \to \beta$ does NOT imply $\beta \to \alpha$.

Reveal Solution
Q6: meaning of α → β ; + counterexample

Meaning: ∀ tuples t1, t2: if t1[α] = t2[α] then t1[β] = t2[β]. // β is a function of α
Counter: student(student_id, student_name)
  student_id → student_name   holds
  student_name → student_id   holds → two "John Smith"s with different IDs.
→ FDs are directional, not symmetric.
Mirrors: A4 Q1, expanded for final scope. Difficulty: Basic.

Your Answer

$\alpha \to \beta$ means: for any two tuples $t_1, t_2$, if $t_1[\alpha] = t_2[\alpha]$ then $t_1[\beta] = t_2[\beta]$. The value of $\beta$ is a function of the value of $\alpha$.

Counterexample. In student(student_id, student_name): student_id $\to$ student_name holds (IDs are unique). But student_name $\to$ student_id fails: two students can share a name ("John Smith" could map to 1001 or 2003).

Understanding

FDs are directional, not symmetric. The reverse arrow needs an independent check.

Part B: SQL Graduation (Q7 to Q9; 32 marks)

Question 7 10 marks SQL

Hospital schema.

patient(patient_id, patient_name, patient_city, age)
doctor(doctor_id, doctor_name, specialty)
appointment(patient_id, doctor_id, appt_date, fee)
(a) (3 marks) Find the names of patients who have had an appointment with a doctor whose specialty is 'Cardiology'.
(b) (3 marks) List each specialty and the average fee for appointments in that specialty. Include only specialties with average fee > 200.
(c) (4 marks) Find the names of patients who have had more appointments than the overall average number of appointments per patient.
Reveal Solution
Q7(a): patients with Cardiology appt // 3-table join

select distinct p.patient_name
from patient p, doctor d, appointment a
where p.patient_id = a.patient_id
and a.doctor_id = d.doctor_id
and d.specialty = 'Cardiology' ;
Q7(b): specialties w/ avg fee > 200 // group + having (aggregate → having, not where)

select d.specialty, avg(a.fee) as avg_fee
from doctor d, appointment a
where d.doctor_id = a.doctor_id
group by d.specialty
having avg(a.fee) > 200 ;
Q7(c): patients w/ more appts than avg // nested aggregate; *1.0 for float

select p.patient_name
from patient p, appointment a
where p.patient_id = a.patient_id
group by p.patient_id, p.patient_name
having count(*) > (
select count(*) * 1.0 / count(distinct patient_id)
from appointment
) ;
Mirrors: A2 Q7 + Midterm Q6-Q7. Hospital is structurally analogous to the Employee-Works-Company trio. Difficulty: Medium.

(a) Your Answer

select distinct p.patient_name
from patient as p, doctor as d, appointment as a
where p.patient_id = a.patient_id
  and a.doctor_id = d.doctor_id
  and d.specialty = 'Cardiology';

Understanding. Three-table join across patient, appointment, doctor. distinct deduplicates patients with multiple cardiology appointments.

(b) Your Answer

select d.specialty, avg(a.fee) as avg_fee
from doctor as d, appointment as a
where d.doctor_id = a.doctor_id
group by d.specialty
having avg(a.fee) > 200;

Understanding. The signature GROUP BY with threshold pattern. Aggregate avg(a.fee) must go in having, not where (where filters rows before grouping; having filters groups after).

(c) Your Answer

select p.patient_name
from patient as p, appointment as a
where p.patient_id = a.patient_id
group by p.patient_id, p.patient_name
having count(*) > (
    select count(*) * 1.0 / count(distinct patient_id)
    from appointment
);

Understanding. Two aggregates. Outer counts this patient's appointments. Inner computes global average (total appointments divided by distinct patients). The * 1.0 forces floating-point division so integer truncation does not bite.

Question 8 12 marks SQL

Airline schema.

staff(staff_id, staff_name, staff_city)
airport(airport_code, airport_city)
route(staff_id, airport_code, flights_assigned)
supervises(staff_id, supervisor_id)
(a) (3 marks) Find staff who live in the same city as their supervisor.
(b) (4 marks) Create a VIEW named busy_staff listing staff_id, staff_name, and total flights_assigned across all airports for staff with total flights $\geq 50$.
(c) (5 marks) Find staff whose flights_assigned at every airport is strictly greater than that of staff with staff_id = 100. Use ALL or NOT EXISTS.
Reveal Solution
Q8(a): staff living in supervisor's city // self-join: alias staff twice

select s1.staff_name
from staff s1, supervises sp, staff s2
where s1.staff_id = sp.staff_id
and sp.supervisor_id = s2.staff_id
and s1.staff_city = s2.staff_city ;
Q8(b): view busy_staff (total flights ≥ 50) // wrap select in create view

create view busy_staff as
select s.staff_id, s.staff_name, sum(r.flights_assigned) as total_flights
from staff s, route r
where s.staff_id = r.staff_id
group by s.staff_id, s.staff_name
having sum(r.flights_assigned) >= 50 ;
Q8(c): staff beating 100 at every airport // ∀ → double NOT EXISTS

select s.staff_name
from staff s
where s.staff_id <> 100
and not exists (
select *
from route r100
where r100.staff_id = 100
  and not exists (
    select * from route r
    where r.staff_id = s.staff_id
      and r.airport_code = r100.airport_code
      and r.flights_assigned > r100.flights_assigned)) ;
pattern: "for all x in B, P(x)" ≡ "NOT EXISTS x in B: NOT P(x)".
Mirrors: A2 Q7 + A3 Q3 + Midterm Q9. Supervises mirrors the manages relation; part (c) mirrors the ">= ALL" pattern. Difficulty: Medium-Hard.

(a) Your Answer

select s1.staff_name
from staff as s1, supervises as sp, staff as s2
where s1.staff_id = sp.staff_id
  and sp.supervisor_id = s2.staff_id
  and s1.staff_city = s2.staff_city;

Understanding. Self-join trick: alias staff twice. s1 is the subordinate; s2 is the supervisor; supervises connects them.

(b) Your Answer

create view busy_staff as
select s.staff_id, s.staff_name, sum(r.flights_assigned) as total_flights
from staff as s, route as r
where s.staff_id = r.staff_id
group by s.staff_id, s.staff_name
having sum(r.flights_assigned) >= 50;

Understanding. Any select can be wrapped in create view. Sum flights across airports (group by the staff key), then restrict totals with having.

(c) Your Answer

select s.staff_name
from staff as s
where s.staff_id <> 100
  and not exists (
      select *
      from route as r100
      where r100.staff_id = 100
        and not exists (
            select *
            from route as r
            where r.staff_id = s.staff_id
              and r.airport_code = r100.airport_code
              and r.flights_assigned > r100.flights_assigned
        )
  );

Understanding. Universal quantification: "for every airport where 100 has an assignment, this staff has strictly more" translates to a double-nested NOT EXISTS. Pattern: "for all $x$ in $B$, $P(x)$" $\equiv$ "NOT EXISTS $x$ in $B$ where NOT $P(x)$".

Question 9 10 marks Division

Supplier schema.

supplier(supplier_id, supplier_name, supplier_city)
department(dept_id, dept_name, dept_city)
supplies(supplier_id, dept_id, item_count)
(a) (4 marks) Find suppliers who supply items to at least one department in 'Calgary'.
(b) (6 marks) Find suppliers who supply to every department (universal quantification). Use NOT EXISTS with EXCEPT.
Reveal Solution
Q9(a): suppliers serving ≥1 Calgary dept // 3-table join + city filter

select distinct s.supplier_name
from supplier s, supplies sup, department d
where s.supplier_id = sup.supplier_id
and sup.dept_id = d.dept_id
and d.dept_city = 'Calgary' ;
Q9(b): suppliers serving every dept // A ⊇ B ≡ NOT EXISTS (B EXCEPT A)

select s.supplier_name
from supplier s
where not exists (
(select dept_id from department)
except
(select sup.dept_id
 from supplies sup
 where sup.supplier_id = s.supplier_id)
) ;
→ B = all dept_ids; A = dept_ids this supplier serves. If B−A empty → covers all.
Mirrors: A3 Q3(b) (companies in every city where IBM is). Difficulty: Hard.

(a) Your Answer

select distinct s.supplier_name
from supplier as s, supplies as sup, department as d
where s.supplier_id = sup.supplier_id
  and sup.dept_id = d.dept_id
  and d.dept_city = 'Calgary';

Understanding. Existential quantifier = three-table join + city filter. Dedupe with distinct because one supplier can serve many Calgary departments.

(b) Your Answer

select s.supplier_name
from supplier as s
where not exists (
    (select dept_id from department)
    except
    (select sup.dept_id
     from supplies as sup
     where sup.supplier_id = s.supplier_id)
);

Understanding. Canonical "$A$ contains $B \equiv$ NOT EXISTS ($B$ EXCEPT $A$)" template. $B$ = all department ids; $A$ = department ids this supplier serves. If $B - A$ is empty, the supplier covers every department.

Question 10 12 marks RA + DDL

Researcher-Papers schema.

researcher(rid, name, affiliation, years_active)
paper(pid, title, authors, venue)
cites(rid, pid, citation_date)
(a) (2 marks) RA: researchers who cited a paper published in venue 'Nature'.
(b) (3 marks) RA: researchers who cited every 'Nature' paper (use division).
(c) (4 marks) RA: for each venue, researchers who cited more than 5 papers from that venue (grouping + count).
(d) (3 marks) SQL DDL: CREATE TABLE for the cites relation with PK and FK constraints.
Reveal Solution
Q10(a): RA, cited a Nature paper

πname(researcher ⋈ cites ⋈ σvenue='Nature'(paper))
Q10(b): cited every Nature paper // "every" → division

T = πrid, pid(cites) ÷ πpid(σvenue='Nature'(paper))
result = πname(researcher ⋈ T)
Q10(c): researchers who cited >5 papers per venue // grouping + count

J = cites ⋈ paper
G = venue, ridγcount-distinct(pid) → cnt(J)
result = πname, venue(researcher ⋈ σcnt>5(G))
Q10(d): DDL for cites // composite PK; cascade on delete

create table cites (
rid            integer not null,
pid            integer not null,
citation_date date,
primary key (rid, pid),
foreign key (rid) references researcher(rid) on delete cascade,
foreign key (pid) references paper(pid) on delete cascade
) ;
Mirrors: A1 Q7 + A2 Q1 + Midterm Q8 (Library-analog). Difficulty: Medium-Hard.

(a) Your Answer

$$\pi_{\text{name}}\left( \text{researcher} \bowtie \text{cites} \bowtie \sigma_{\text{venue} = \text{'Nature'}}(\text{paper}) \right).$$

Understanding. Natural joins line up on shared attributes (rid between researcher and cites; pid between cites and paper). Filter paper to Nature before joining to shrink intermediate results.

(b) Your Answer

$$T = \pi_{\text{rid}, \text{pid}}(\text{cites}) \div \pi_{\text{pid}}\!\left(\sigma_{\text{venue} = \text{'Nature'}}(\text{paper})\right),$$ $$\text{result} = \pi_{\text{name}}(\text{researcher} \bowtie T).$$

Understanding. Division returns rids that appear paired with every Nature pid. Join back with researcher to translate rid into name.

(c) Your Answer

Let $J = \text{cites} \bowtie \text{paper}$. Then $$G = {}_{\text{venue, rid}}\gamma_{\text{count-distinct}(\text{pid}) \to \text{cnt}}(J),$$ $$\text{result} = \pi_{\text{name}, \text{venue}}\!\left( \text{researcher} \bowtie \sigma_{\text{cnt} \gt 5}(G) \right).$$

Understanding. Group by (venue, rid) so the count is per researcher per venue. Filter counts > 5. Join back for name.

(d) Your Answer

create table cites (
    rid             integer not null,
    pid             integer not null,
    citation_date   date,
    primary key (rid, pid),
    foreign key (rid) references researcher(rid) on delete cascade,
    foreign key (pid) references paper(pid) on delete cascade
);

Understanding. PK of an M:N relation is the composite of the two side PKs. Cascade on delete because a citation has no meaning without its researcher or paper.

Question 11 6 marks RA $\to$ SQL

Given $R(A, B, C)$ and $S(B, C, D)$, translate each RA expression to SQL.

(a) (2 marks) $\pi_{A, D}\!\left(\sigma_{A = \text{'x'}}(R \bowtie S)\right)$
(b) (2 marks) $\pi_A(R) - \pi_A\!\left(\sigma_{D = \text{'y'}}(S)\right)$
(c) (2 marks) $R \cap S$
Reveal Solution
Q11(a): πA,D(σA='x'(R ⋈ S))

select r.A, s.D
from R r, S s
where r.A = 'x'
and r.B = s.B
and r.C = s.C ;
Q11(b): πA(R) − πA(σD='y'(S)) // − → EXCEPT

(select A from R)
except
(select R.A from R, S
where R.B=S.B and R.C=S.C and S.D='y') ;
Q11(c): R ∩ S // ← FLAG: schemas differ; need union-compatible!

NOTE R(A,B,C) and S(B,C,D) → not union-compatible.
Intended: intersection on shared (B, C):

(select B, C from R)
intersect
(select B, C from S) ;
→ the flag is part of the marks.
Mirrors: A2 Q3-Q5. Standard RA-to-SQL translation drill. Difficulty: Medium.

(a) Your Answer

select r.A, s.D
from R as r, S as s
where r.A = 'x'
  and r.B = s.B
  and r.C = s.C;

Understanding. Natural join on the shared attributes (B, C). Selection becomes where; projection becomes the select list.

(b) Your Answer

(select A from R)
except
(select R.A from R, S where R.B = S.B and R.C = S.C and S.D = 'y');

Understanding. Set difference in SQL is except. The right side must identify which $R$-rows are linked to a $D = $ 'y' row of $S$, then remove those $A$-values from $\pi_A(R)$.

(c) Your Answer

Note: $R(A, B, C)$ and $S(B, C, D)$ have different schemas; $R \cap S$ requires union-compatible operands (same arity and compatible columns). The intended reading is intersection on the shared attributes $(B, C)$:

(select B, C from R)
intersect
(select B, C from S);

Understanding. Set operators $\cup, \cap, -$ require union-compatible schemas. Flagging this mismatch is part of the answer; the prof wants you to catch it explicitly.

Part D: E-R Design (Q12 to Q13; 23 marks)

Question 12 15 marks ER + ISA

Music streaming service. Design requirements:

  • users: user_id, email, join_date, country.
  • artists: artist_id, name, country_of_origin.
  • songs: song_id, title, duration, release_year.
  • Users create playlists (playlist_id, name, created_date) and add songs.
  • Users can subscribe to artists (notifications).
  • Each song has exactly one primary artist and may have multiple featured artists.
  • Each user has exactly one subscription plan: Free (ad_frequency), Premium (billing_cycle), or Family (family_size).
(a) (5 marks) Construct an E-R diagram: entities, relationships (cardinality + participation), PK attributes.
(b) (5 marks) Handle the multi-valued "featured artists" attribute properly. Explain your design choice.
(c) (5 marks) Add the subscription plans using generalization / ISA. Show the ISA and list shared vs specific attributes.
Reveal Solution
Q12(a): entities + PKs
user       (user_id, email, join_date, country)
artist     (artist_id, name, country_of_origin)
song       (song_id, title, duration, release_year)
playlist   (playlist_id, name, created_date)
Rels / cards / part.: 
creates       user --1:N--> playlist     (playlist: total)
contains      playlist M:N  song          (partial both)
subscribes_to user M:N  artist            (partial)
performs      artist --1:N--> song      (song: total, primary)
features      artist M:N  song            (partial, featured)
Q12(b): featured-artist multi-valued // model as M:N rel, NOT a string/multivalued attr
→ features(song_id, artist_id) junction.
  rationale: (i) clean M:N, (ii) 1NF-safe, (iii) no double-oval to flatten later.
Q12(c): plan ISA
Supertype subscription(sub_id, plan_type)
  ISA ↓ three subtypes:
  - Free → ad_frequency
  - Premium → billing_cycle
  - Family → family_size
disjoint + total constraint.
user —has— subscription (1:1, total on user).
Mirrors: A3 Q4 + A3 Q8(c) (generalization with multiple subtypes). Difficulty: Medium-Hard.

(a) Your Answer

Entity sets and PKs:

  • user: user_id, email, join_date, country.
  • artist: artist_id, name, country_of_origin.
  • song: song_id, title, duration, release_year.
  • playlist: playlist_id, name, created_date.

Relationships (cardinality; participation):

  • creates (user $\to$ playlist): 1:N; playlist has total participation.
  • contains (playlist $\leftrightarrow$ song): M:N; partial on both.
  • subscribes_to (user $\leftrightarrow$ artist): M:N; partial.
  • performs (artist $\to$ song, primary): 1:N; song total (every song has exactly one primary artist).
  • features (artist $\leftrightarrow$ song, featured): M:N; partial.

Understanding. Separate "performs" (primary, 1:N) from "features" (featured, M:N) because they have different cardinalities.

(b) Your Answer

Design choice: model featured artists as a separate M:N relationship features(song_id, artist_id).

When mapped to relational, this becomes a junction table song_featured(song_id, artist_id) with composite PK {song_id, artist_id} and foreign keys to song and artist.

Rationale. Featured artists are multi-valued, and each value is an entity (not a simple attribute). Storing as a relationship (i) captures M:N cardinality cleanly, (ii) avoids a non-atomic comma-separated string column (which would violate 1NF), (iii) avoids a double-ellipse multi-valued attribute bubble that would still need flattening on conversion.

Understanding. Multi-valued attributes whose values are themselves entities are always best modeled as relationships.

(c) Your Answer

Introduce a supertype subscription with shared attribute subscription_id (PK) and discriminator plan_type. Each user has a 1:1 has relationship to subscription (total on user side).

ISA triangle to three subtypes:

  • Free: specific attribute ad_frequency.
  • Premium: specific attribute billing_cycle.
  • Family: specific attribute family_size.

Constraints: disjoint (a subscription is in exactly one subtype) and total (every subscription must be in some subtype).

Understanding. Exactly the pattern from A3 Q8(c) with three children instead of two. When mapped to relational form, you can use (i) one table per subtype with supertype attributes repeated, or (ii) one table per entity with a discriminator column. The prof does not require you to pick a mapping style unless asked.

Question 13 8 marks ER to Relational

Given E-R description. E-commerce: Customer(customer_id, email); Order(order_id, date); Product(sku, price); places (1:N from Customer to Order); contains (M:N between Order and Product with quantity).

(a) (4 marks) Convert this ER to a relational schema. List all tables with PKs underlined.
(b) (4 marks) Identify all foreign keys and referential constraints. State an ON DELETE action for each.
Reveal Solution
Q13(a): ER → relational // 1:N merged; M:N → its own table
customer(customer_id, email)
orders  (order_id, date, customer_id)   // 1:N places merged in
product (sku, price)
contains(order_id, sku, quantity)     // M:N junction
→ "orders" plural because ORDER is a SQL reserved word.
Q13(b): FKs + referential actions
orders.customer_id → customer(customer_id)   ON DELETE RESTRICT
contains.order_id  → orders(order_id)         ON DELETE CASCADE
contains.sku       → product(sku)              ON DELETE RESTRICT
reasons:
  RESTRICT cust: don't drop a cust w/ active orders.
  CASCADE line items: dependent on their parent order.
  RESTRICT sku: preserve sales history.
Mirrors: A3 Q8(a) (entity sets + relationship FKs table). Difficulty: Basic-Medium.

(a) Your Answer

TableSchema (PK underlined)
customer(customer_id, email)

Question 14 10 marks FD Theory

Given $R = (A, B, C, D, E, F)$ with $F = \{A \to B,\ B \to CD,\ D \to E,\ AF \to B\}$.

(a) (3 marks) Compute $(AF)^+$.
(b) (3 marks) List all candidate keys of $R$.
(c) (4 marks) Prove pseudo-transitivity using Armstrong's axioms: if $\alpha \to \beta$ and $\beta\gamma \to \delta$, then $\alpha\gamma \to \delta$. Number every step and cite the axiom.
Reveal Solution

start:          {A, F}

other {X,F}: B+F = {B,C,D,E,F} miss A ✗; C+F = {C,F} ✗; D+F = {D,E,F} ✗; E+F = {E,F} ✗.

Mirrors: A4 Q3 + Q4 + Q6(a) (closure + candidate key + Armstrong proof). Difficulty: Medium.

(b) Your Answer

{A, F} is a superkey since $(AF)^+ = R$. Check minimality:

  • $A^+ = \{A, B, C, D, E\}$ (missing $F$); not a superkey.
  • $F^+ = \{F\}$; not a superkey.

So {A, F} is minimal. Since $F$ never appears on any RHS, $F$ must be in every candidate key. Testing {B, F}, {C, F}, {D, F}, {E, F} individually none reach $R$ (all miss $A$, $B$, or similar).

(c) Your Answer

Claim. If $\alpha \to \beta$ and $\beta\gamma \to \delta$, then $\alpha\gamma \to \delta$.

Understanding. Pseudo-transitivity is a derived rule, so we assemble it from the three primitives (reflexivity, augmentation, transitivity). Augmentation bridges from $\alpha \to \beta$ to $\alpha\gamma \to \beta\gamma$; transitivity chains into $\delta$.

Question 15 15 marks BCNF Showpiece

Given $R = (A, B, C, D, E)$ with $F = \{AB \to C,\ C \to D,\ D \to E,\ E \to A\}$.

(a) (4 marks) Compute the candidate keys. Justify by attribute closure.
(b) (4 marks) Identify every BCNF violation. Justify each.
(c) (5 marks) Losslessly decompose $R$ into BCNF. Show each step.
(d) (2 marks) Is your decomposition dependency-preserving? Justify.
Reveal Solution
Q15(a): candidate keys // B never on RHS → B in every CK
(AB)+: {A,B} →AB→C→ {A,B,C} →C→D→ {A,B,C,D} →D→E→ R       ✓ CK

Mirrors: A4 Q5 + Q6 (full BCNF decomposition with lossless + dependency-preserving check). Difficulty: Hard.

(a) Your Answer

$B$ appears on no RHS, so $B$ is in every candidate key.

  • $(AB)^+$: $\{A, B\} \xrightarrow{AB \to C} \{A,B,C\} \xrightarrow{C \to D} \{A,B,C,D\} \xrightarrow{D \to E} R$. $AB$ is a CK.
  • $(BC)^+$: $\{B,C\} \xrightarrow{C \to D} \{B,C,D\} \xrightarrow{D \to E} \{B,C,D,E\} \xrightarrow{E \to A} R$. $BC$ is a CK.
  • $(BD)^+$: $\{B,D\} \xrightarrow{D \to E} \{B,D,E\} \xrightarrow{E \to A} \{A,B,D,E\} \xrightarrow{AB \to C} R$. $BD$ is a CK.
  • $(BE)^+$: $\{B,E\} \xrightarrow{E \to A} \{A,B,E\} \xrightarrow{AB \to C} \{A,B,C,E\} \xrightarrow{C \to D} R$. $BE$ is a CK.

Candidate keys: {AB, BC, BD, BE}.

Understanding. The chain $C \to D \to E \to A$ loops back through $AB \to C$, so each of $A, C, D, E$ can reach the others once paired with $B$.

(b) Your Answer

  • $AB \to C$: $AB$ is a CK. No violation.
  • $C \to D$: $C^+$ in $R$ = $\{A, C, D, E\}$, missing $B$; $C$ is NOT a superkey. Violation.
  • $D \to E$: $D^+$ = $\{A, D, E\}$, missing $B, C$; NOT a superkey. Violation.
  • $E \to A$: $E^+$ = $\{A, E\}$, missing $B, C, D$; NOT a superkey. Violation.

(c) Your Answer

Step 1: decompose on $C \to D$. $C^+_R = \{A, C, D, E\}$.

R_1 = (A, C, D, E)
R_2 = (B, C)

Check $R_1$. FDs on $R_1$: $C \to D$, $D \to E$, $E \to A$ (plus derived $C \to E$, $C \to A$, $D \to A$). Unique CK of $R_1$ is $\{C\}$ ($C^+ = ACDE$). Test BCNF:

  • $C \to D$: $C$ is CK. OK.
  • $D \to E$: $D^+ = \{A, D, E\}$, missing $C$; NOT a superkey. Violation.

Step 2: decompose $R_1$ on $D \to E$. $D^+_{R_1} = \{A, D, E\}$.

R_1a = (A, D, E)
R_1b = (C, D)

Check $R_{1a}$. FDs: $D \to E$, $E \to A$, derived $D \to A$. Unique CK = $\{D\}$. Test: $D \to E$ OK; $E \to A$: $E^+ = \{A, E\}$, missing $D$; NOT a superkey. Violation.

Step 3: decompose $R_{1a}$ on $E \to A$.

R_1a_alpha = (A, E)
R_1a_beta  = (D, E)

Check all leaves:

  • $R_{1a\alpha} = (A, E)$: $E \to A$; $E$ is CK. BCNF.
  • $R_{1a\beta} = (D, E)$: $D \to E$; $D$ is CK. BCNF.
  • $R_{1b} = (C, D)$: $C \to D$; $C$ is CK. BCNF.
  • $R_2 = (B, C)$: no non-trivial FDs. BCNF trivially.

Final decomposition: $\{(A, E),\ (D, E),\ (C, D),\ (B, C)\}$.

Understanding. At each decomposition the intersection of the two fragments contains the LHS of the violator, which guarantees lossless join by the standard BCNF theorem.

(d) Your Answer

  • $C \to D$: $C, D$ in $(C, D)$. Preserved.
  • $D \to E$: $D, E$ in $(D, E)$. Preserved.
  • $E \to A$: $E, A$ in $(A, E)$. Preserved.
  • $AB \to C$: $A \in (A, E)$, $B \in (B, C)$, $C \in (C, D)$. No single fragment has all three; NOT preserved.

The decomposition is NOT dependency-preserving.

Understanding. Classic BCNF trade-off: BCNF always gives lossless join but may sacrifice dependency preservation. The alternative is 3NF (always dependency-preserving but may keep some transitive dependencies).

(a) (4 marks) Draw a brief ER diagram.
(b) (3 marks) Give a reasonable set of FDs.
(c) (3 marks) Check whether your schema is in BCNF. If not, decompose.

  • enrolls student $\leftrightarrow$ course, M:N, descriptive attribute grade. Partial on both sides.
  • teaches instructor $\to$ course, 1:N (one instructor per course; instructor teaches many courses). Total on course (every course has an instructor).

(b) Your Answer

  • course_id $\to$ course_title, credits, instructor_id.

  • student_id, course_id $\to$ grade.

  • course(course_id, course_title, credits, instructor_id). FD LHS is CK. BCNF.
  • instructor(instructor_id, name, department). FD LHS is CK. BCNF.
  • enrollment(student_id, course_id, grade). FD LHS is CK. BCNF.

Scoring the Simulation

After you finish

Tally your marks out of 120. Then compare to this diagnostic:

  • 100+: strong A territory; final-week prep should be review-only (formula sheet, Professor's Signature page, and a skim of Part F walkthroughs).
  • 85-99: solid B; identify which category you lost marks in (SQL, RA, ER, BCNF) and hit the corresponding walkthrough page once more.
  • 70-84: you have all the pieces but the recall is slow or the details are sloppy. Timed retries of this practice exam plus drilling the formula sheet is the right move.
  • Below 70: go back to Part C (walkthroughs) with the specific category page and re-do the examples by hand.

Do not calibrate to an easier practice; calibrate to the prof's style. The real final will feel familiar if you have worked this page all the way through, including the Reveals, at least twice.

Remember: paper-based

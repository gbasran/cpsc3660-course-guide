Entity-Relationship Model & Diagrams

Designing the schema before you write any SQL; entities, attributes, relationships, and how to translate them into tables.

Part A: Entities (Strong and Weak)

Physical analogy; a family tree on the wall.

Look at a family tree. Each person is drawn as a rectangle with a name inside; they are entities. A person can stand on their own; given just "Ada Lovelace, born 1815" you know who is being discussed. That is a strong entity. But then there is a side-box that says "Ada's pet cat, Mr. Whiskers"; Mr. Whiskers the cat only makes sense if you also know which human he belongs to. "Mr. Whiskers" alone is ambiguous; maybe ten households have a cat named Mr. Whiskers. So on the diagram, his box is drawn with a double border to signal "I cannot identify myself; I need my owner." That is a weak entity, and it always attaches to a strong entity through an identifying relationship (also drawn with a double border). The cat contributes a discriminator (his name, unique within the household) and the household contributes the rest of the identification.
Definition: Entity types.
Entity
A real-world thing the database needs to remember. Drawn as a rectangle with the type name inside.
Strong entity
Can be identified by its own attributes alone; has a primary key of its own. Single-line rectangle.
Weak entity
Cannot be identified by its own attributes; needs a strong owner. Drawn with a double-line rectangle.
Identifying relationship
The relationship that connects a weak entity to its owner. Drawn as a double-line diamond. Participation of the weak entity is always total (thick line).
Discriminator (partial key)
Attribute(s) of the weak entity that are unique within a single owner. Drawn with a dashed underline. The weak entity's full primary key is (owner's PK) + discriminator.

Modeling Payments for a Loan (classic weak-entity example)

Step 1: Identify the entities

A loan has a unique loan_number; it is a strong entity. A payment is made toward a specific loan; each payment has a payment_number starting from 1 for that loan. Payment #1 for loan L-17 and payment #1 for loan L-18 are different payments; the number alone does not identify.

Step 2: Classify and draw

loan: strong entity, single rectangle, primary key loan_number.
payment: weak entity, double rectangle, discriminator payment_number, also amount and date.
loan_payment: identifying relationship, double diamond, connecting them.

Step 3: Full primary key of the weak entity

When we map to relations, payment becomes:

payment(loan_number, payment_number, amount, date)

The primary key is the owner's PK plus the discriminator. loan_number is also a foreign key back to loan.

Weak entity rule: PK = owner.PK ∪ discriminator.

Exam Pattern (A3 Q7)

The prof has asked "justify why payment is modeled as a weak entity." Good answer: "payment_number alone is not unique across all loans; without knowing which loan owns it, we cannot pick out a specific payment; therefore payment cannot identify itself and must borrow loan_number from its owner." Always name the identifying relationship and the discriminator explicitly.

Part B: Attributes

Physical analogy; the fields on a driver's licence.

Your licence has several kinds of fields. The licence number is a single value written once; that is a simple attribute. Your full name is printed as one string but logically it splits into first / middle / last; three sub-parts of one attribute, a composite. Under emergency contacts you can list up to three people; one attribute, many values, a multivalued. And nowhere is your age printed, but your date of birth is; anyone can compute age from birthdate + today, so age is derived. Four distinct flavors, one mental picture.
Definition: Attribute types (with diagram conventions).
Simple (atomic)
Single indivisible value. Drawn as an ellipse connected to the entity. e.g., age, empid.
Composite
Decomposes into sub-attributes. The parent ellipse has child ellipses branching off it. e.g., name(first, middle, last), address(street, city, postal).
Multivalued
Can hold a set of values for a single entity. Drawn as a double ellipse. e.g., phone_numbers, authors of a book.
Derived
Computed from other attributes or context; not stored. Drawn as a dashed ellipse. e.g., age derived from dob.
Key attribute
Underlined ellipse; marks the primary identifier.

Attribute decomposition for employee

Step 1: Starting ER description

"An employee has an empid, a full name (first, middle, last), a set of phone numbers, an age computed from their date of birth."

Step 3: When mapping to relations (preview)

Composite: flatten to atomic columns: first_name, middle_name, last_name. (See A3 Q6.)
Multivalued: create a separate table emp_phone(empid, phone_number) with both columns as the primary key. (See A3 Q5.)
Derived: do not store; compute on demand in a query or view.

The prof specifically tests "decompose this composite" and "separate this multivalued"; see A3 Q5 and A3 Q6.

Exam Pattern (A3 Q5, A3 Q6)

Given an ER with a multivalued attribute authors on books, you will be asked to produce the relational schema. Correct answer has TWO tables: books(isbn, title, publisher, ...) and book_authors(isbn, author) with (isbn, author) as the PK of the second table, and isbn as a FK back. Never put a comma-separated list of authors inside one column.

Part C: Relationships

Physical analogy; the lines on the family tree.

Two rectangles sitting side-by-side on the wall mean nothing by themselves. It is the line drawn between them, labeled "married to" or "parent of," that gives the structure meaning. In ER diagrams the line is drawn as a diamond with the relationship name inside, connected to the rectangles it relates. A relationship can connect two entities (binary), three (ternary), or more, and we decorate each connecting line with two pieces of information: the cardinality (how many of the other side each side can have; 1:1, 1:N, or M:N) and the participation (does every entity have to participate? total vs partial).
Definition: Relationship anatomy.
Degree
How many entity types participate. Binary (2), ternary (3), n-ary.
Cardinality ratio
1:1 each entity on either side has at most one partner. 1:N one entity on the "1" side pairs with many on the "N" side, but each on "N" has at most one on "1." M:N both sides are many.
Participation (total vs partial)
Total participation means every entity of that type must participate in the relationship (thick line); partial means participation is optional (thin line).
Role names
Labels on the connecting lines; needed when an entity plays more than one role (e.g., employee as "manager" vs "subordinate" in a manages relationship).
Descriptive attributes
Attributes that belong to the relationship itself, not to either entity; e.g., borrow_date belongs to borrows, not to employee or book.

Cardinality for employee-borrows-books

Step 1: State the business rule

An employee may borrow many books; a book may be borrowed by many employees (over time); every borrow event has a date. Therefore borrows is M:N between employee and books, and has a descriptive attribute borrow_date.

Step 2: Draw it

Rectangles: employee on the left, books on the right. Diamond in the middle labeled borrows. An ellipse borrow_date hangs off the diamond. Both connecting lines are thin (partial participation; some employees never borrow, some books never get borrowed).

Step 3: Map to a relation

Because borrows is M:N, we need its own table (we cannot fold it into either entity's table):

borrow(empid, isbn, borrow_date)
  foreign key (empid) references employee(empid)
  foreign key (isbn) references books(isbn)

M:N always becomes its own relation. 1:N folds the FK into the "N" side. 1:1 can go either way.

Part D: Generalization / ISA Hierarchies

Physical analogy; the taxonomy of animals.

"Dog," "cat," and "horse" are all species, but they share the general traits of "mammal" (warm-blooded, has fur, breathes air). Rather than repeat those traits on every species box, we introduce a parent box called mammal and draw an arrow from each specific-kind box up to it, labeled ISA (read: "is a"). Dog ISA mammal. Cat ISA mammal. The specific species inherit the general traits and add their own. In an ER diagram, the parent entity sits above the children, connected through a triangle labeled "ISA" with arrows pointing upward.
Definition: Generalization / Specialization.
Superclass / superentity
The general entity with shared attributes; e.g., person(ssn, name, address).
Subclass / subentity
A specialization of the superclass with extra attributes; e.g., student(gpa), employee(salary).
ISA relationship
Drawn as a triangle labeled ISA. Inheritance: every subclass instance is also a superclass instance.
Disjoint vs Overlapping
Disjoint: a superclass instance belongs to at most one subclass. Overlapping: may belong to several (a person could be both student and employee).
Total vs Partial
Total: every superclass instance must appear in some subclass. Partial: some superclass instances belong to no subclass.

Exam Pattern (A3 Q8(c))

A bookstore sells both printed books and e-books. Both share isbn, title, publisher, price; printed books also have weight, shipping_cost; e-books have file_format, download_url. Model with book as the superclass and printed_book, e_book as subclasses connected by an ISA triangle. State whether disjoint or overlapping (a physical book is not also an e-book, so disjoint) and whether total or partial (every book in the store is one kind, so total). Justify in one sentence.

Part E: ER → Relational Mapping

Physical analogy; translating a blueprint into carpentry cuts.

An architect draws a house on paper; rectangles for rooms, lines for walls, symbols for doors and windows. A carpenter looks at that blueprint and translates it into a cut list: "I need seven 2x4s of length 96 inches, four sheets of drywall, three doors." ER-to-relational mapping is the same kind of translation. You look at rectangles, diamonds, and ellipses and you produce CREATE TABLE statements, with specific rules for each ER element. The rules are mechanical; if you follow them step by step, you get a correct schema every time.
Rule: Mapping each ER element to relations.
Strong entity
Becomes its own table. Attributes become columns. The key attribute becomes the primary key.
Weak entity
Becomes its own table. Columns = its own attributes + the owner's primary key. PK = owner PK + discriminator. FK = owner PK, with ON DELETE CASCADE.
1:1 relationship
Add the PK of one side as a FK (and UNIQUE) on the other side. Either side works; pick the one with total participation.
1:N relationship
Add the PK of the "1" side as a FK on the "N" side. No new table needed.
M:N relationship
Create a new table. Columns = PKs of both sides + any descriptive attributes. PK = both FKs combined.
Ternary / n-ary relationship
Create a new table. Columns = PKs of all participating entities + descriptive attributes. PK usually = all participating PKs combined.
Multivalued attribute
Create a separate table. Columns = owner PK + the multivalued attribute. PK = both.
Composite attribute
Decompose into atomic sub-attributes (columns) in the same table. Do not create a separate table.
Derived attribute
Do NOT store. Compute at query time or use a view.
ISA (generalization)
Three options. (1) One table per subclass only, replicating parent attributes. (2) One table per class, each subclass table has the superclass PK as both PK and FK. (3) One big table with all attributes and a "type" column; nulls where inapplicable. Option (2) is the textbook-standard.

Full mapping of the library ER

Step 1: List the ER elements

Entities: employee (strong, PK empid), books (strong, PK isbn) with multivalued attribute authors. Relationship: borrows (M:N, with borrow_date).

Step 2: Apply the rules

Strong entity employee → one table.
Strong entity books with multivalued authors → two tables; books itself (sans authors) and book_authors.
M:N borrows → one table.

Step 3: Write the schema
employee(empid, name, office, age)

books(isbn, title, publisher)

book_authors(isbn, author)
  foreign key (isbn) references books(isbn)

borrow(empid, isbn, borrow_date)
  foreign key (empid) references employee(empid)
  foreign key (isbn) references books(isbn)

Four tables from one ER; notice the multivalued attribute did NOT stay in books.

Why This Matters

Every ER-design exam question ends with "map to a relational schema." The mapping is mechanical, but the common mistake is to leave a multivalued attribute as one column with commas inside. That is not first normal form and loses all points. Always give multivalueds their own table; always put composites as atomic columns in the parent table.

Exam Pattern

Almost every ER question on the final follows the same script: (1) read the business description, (2) draw the ER with correct notation (double rectangles for weak entities, double ellipses for multivalued, double diamonds for identifying relationships, dashed ellipses for derived), (3) translate to relational schema using the rules above. Always show the FK declarations explicitly; do not just underline keys.

Glossary

Entity
A thing the database models. Drawn as a rectangle.
Strong entity
Self-identifying; single-line rectangle; has its own PK.
Weak entity
Needs a strong owner to be identified; double-line rectangle; PK = owner PK + discriminator.
Discriminator (partial key)
Unique within an owner; dashed underline in the diagram.
Identifying relationship
Connects a weak entity to its owner; drawn as a double diamond.
Simple attribute
Atomic single value; ellipse.
Composite attribute
Breaks into sub-attributes; ellipse with children.
Multivalued attribute
Can hold a set of values; double ellipse; maps to a separate table.
Derived attribute
Computed from others; dashed ellipse; never stored.
Cardinality ratio
1:1, 1:N, or M:N; how many partners each side may have.
Total participation
Every entity must participate; thick connecting line.
Partial participation
Participation is optional; thin connecting line.
Role name
Label on a connecting line; needed when an entity plays multiple roles (e.g., self-relationships).
Generalization / ISA
Subclasses specialize a superclass; triangle notation.
Disjoint / overlapping
Whether subclass memberships are exclusive or allow overlap.
Total / partial specialization
Whether every superclass instance must appear in some subclass.
